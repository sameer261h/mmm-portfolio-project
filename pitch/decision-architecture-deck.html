Where Statistics Ends and Judgment Begins

01 / 11
Statistical inference

An MMM doesn't predict a budget. It infers response curves.

"Predicts budget allocation" is the popular shorthand, and it's not quite right. For every channel, the model estimates a small set of physical parameters — how long an ad's effect lingers (adstock decay), how fast that channel hits diminishing returns (saturation), and how strongly spend converts to revenue (a coefficient). Each one comes out as a distribution, not a number.

estimated
Adstock decay, saturation shape, coefficient — per channel
e.g. "Search's coefficient is most plausibly 0.6, but 0.1–1.4 is also consistent with the data"
assumed
Functional form (geometric adstock, logistic saturation), priors, and stationarity
the future behaves enough like the training window to trust it
honest confidence
A well-fit posterior — not a certainty
a credible interval accompanies every single estimate above
02 / 11
Deterministic optimization

Optimization begins after inference ends.

The Bayesian model's job stops at the response curves. A separate, ordinary, deterministic optimizer then solves a constrained problem on top of those curves — nothing more exotic than that, and that simplicity is deliberate: it's auditable and reproducible.

// maximize expected revenue, subject to a fixed budget and business-realism bounds
maximize Σi fi(spendi)
subject to Σi spendi = total_budget
        loweri ≤ spendi ≤ upperi // e.g. "can't 10x TV overnight"

fi is the fitted response curve for channel i — usually evaluated at the posterior mean, which is exactly where the next section's caveat comes from.

03 / 11
Human judgment

The optimizer's answer is a point. The truth is a neighborhood.

Say the optimizer returns Search → ₹8L, PMax → ₹5L, Demand Gen → ₹2L, YouTube → ₹1L. Two structural reasons this isn't executed as-is:

01 — the argmax is itself uncertain
It's the best split of an estimate. Resample the posterior and the "optimal" split shifts — sometimes meaningfully. A point estimate hides that fragility.
02 — it only knows what it was shown
Nothing about a launch next week, a competitor's move, or a supply constraint made it into fi. See the taxonomy, next.

Mature orgs insert a review stage because the failure is asymmetric: money moves and campaigns launch fast; unwinding a bad call is slower than reviewing it first.

04 / 11
Human judgment

What lives outside the model — a taxonomy, not a shrug.

"Ignore the recommendation, increase Search instead" can be entirely correct. Here's every category of knowledge an MMM structurally cannot have, because none of it is a column in the training data:

Commercial calendar
Launches, price changes, promotions not yet in the training window
Competitive dynamics
A rival's spend shift, stockout, exit, or bidding war
Creative & fatigue
Creative quality, audience fatigue, a refresh about to ship
Operational limits
Inventory, fulfillment capacity, sales team bandwidth for a demand spike
Contractual & partner
Minimum spend commitments, negotiated rates, exclusivity terms
Brand & governance
Brand-safety limits, legal/political restrictions, priorities the model can't see in "revenue"
Regime shift
Platform algorithm changes, macro shocks, anything the training window never contained
Everything else, honestly
If it isn't a column, it isn't in fi — full stop
05 / 11
Human judgment

MMM says A, a person says B. Neither side wins by default.

Not "obey the model" and not "obey the human" — an actual arbitration sequence:

01
Locate the claim. Does it fall in the taxonomy above — something structurally outside the model's inputs? If yes, the human's context wins by design, and gets logged as a labeled factor for the next refresh.
02
Size it against the interval. If it's a claim about something the model did see, check whether it sits inside the credible interval. Inside it isn't real disagreement — it's a preference within the model's own uncertainty. Proceed, and log it.
03
Outside the interval, no taxonomy story: test, don't argue. Run the smaller version as a bounded experiment or holdout before committing the full swing. Uncertainty becomes an action, not a stalemate.
04
Log every override, either direction. It becomes a labeled example the next model iteration learns from — this is literally what guardrails.py's per-change cap enforces in the shipped system: no single override, human or automated, moves further than a bounded, logged step.
06 / 11
LLM reasoning

The LLM never touches the arithmetic.

The constrained optimization in section 02 stays deterministic — same inputs, same output, every time, which is what makes it auditable. The LLM's real job sits one layer up: translate, explain, draft, and ask — never compute the number itself.

infers
Bayesian MMM
response curves + uncertainty
→
computes
Deterministic optimizer
constrained budget split
→
translates
LLM
drafts campaigns, explains reasoning, flags contradictions, asks when unclear
→
decides
Human
approves, rejects, or asks for more

Most "AI marketing agent" demos skip this separation and let the model output a budget number directly — the moment that happens, the number stops being reproducible or explainable. That's the mistake this system was built to avoid.

08 / 11
Human judgment

A better posterior shrinks known unknowns. It cannot touch unknown ones.

Suppose the model gets dramatically better — tighter intervals, cleaner MCMC, more data. Three reasons a human still has to be in the loop, and one honest reason they sometimes shouldn't override it:

1
The ceiling on uncertainty. More precision narrows the credible interval on channels already in the model. It teaches the model nothing about a competitor's unannounced launch — see section 04's taxonomy, which doesn't shrink as the math improves.
2
Accountability. A real budget decision needs an owner who can be held responsible for the outcome. That cannot be assigned to a posterior distribution.
3
Model risk is not statistical risk. A sophisticated model can still be wrong in ways it can't self-diagnose — data drift, a silently broken pipeline, a regime change. A second, independent check is a control, not distrust of the math.
✕
When the human should stand down: an unsubstantiated hunch, contradicted by strong, well-identified evidence the model did see, with no taxonomy story behind it. That's when the person updates their belief — not the system.
09 / 11
The full architecture

Designed once, end to end — this is the actual shape.

infers
Bayesian MMM — historical spend/revenue → response curves + a full posterior over every parameter
optimizes
Deterministic constrained optimizer — posterior mean curves → one budget split, bounded by business-realism constraints
reasons
LLM planner / analyst / operator — translates the split into concrete campaigns, answers questions by choosing which read-only tool to call, drafts proposed changes
decides
Human approval gate — every write is reviewed; nothing real happens without an explicit approve
executes
Real API calls — Google Ads + Meta Ads, campaigns always created paused, guardrails enforced in code, not in the prompt
↩ outcomes and every logged override feed back into the next model refresh — the loop closes, it doesn't dead-end

Code tests vs. evals: 60 pytest tests check code correctness — schema validation, guardrail math, mock writes. A separate eval harness (ads_agent/evals.py) scores decision quality on 14 scripted scenarios with known-correct answers, comparing a deterministic fallback against the LLM path — coverage spans both directions of every action the agent can take, restraint under four kinds of temptation, a root-cause-vs-symptom discrimination test, and three data-integrity edge cases (missing data, an empty account, a prompt-injection attempt embedded in account data). It already found a real gap: the deterministic path scores 14/14 every run; the LLM path scores a 33% restraint rate and is fully consistent on only 5 of 14 scenarios across repeated runs, sometimes proposing a change its own stated reasoning says shouldn't trigger one. Reported here on purpose, not glossed over — 14 of a cataloged 60 scenarios are built, with the other 46 documented as an explicit Level 2-4 roadmap, not silently deferred.

11 / 11
The moat

The moat isn't a clever prompt. It's the discipline in sections 02–08.

It is easy to let an LLM output a budget number and call it "agentic." It is much harder — and much rarer — to know exactly which layer a decision belongs in, and build the boring, gated, auditable version instead of the flashy autonomous one.

Next: autonomous initiation — the agent starts its own monitoring runs and earns permission to auto-apply exactly one low-risk, reversible action once the eval suite proves it reliable, never a budget or pause decision (planned, documented, not built — see docs/L4_AUTONOMY_SPEC.md) · Meta Ads' final write step once a region-compatible payment method is in place · outcome memory that checks whether each applied change actually worked and feeds that back into the next decision.
