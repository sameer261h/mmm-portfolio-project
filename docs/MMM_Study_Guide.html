What is MMM?

Marketing Mix Modeling answers the CMO's two questions: how much of each week's sales did each marketing channel cause, and where should the next dollar of budget go?

It's a regression on aggregate weekly data — sales explained by channel spend plus controls (price, promos, holidays, macro). No user-level tracking, which is why MMM demand surged after iOS14 and cookie deprecation broke click attribution.

The model in one line

salest = baseline + Σ βc · saturation( adstock( spendc,t ) ) + Σ γ · controlst + error

The two transforms — adstock (carryover) and saturation (diminishing returns) — are what make it MMM rather than plain regression. Try them in the Concepts Lab tab.

Concepts Lab

Transform 1

Adstock — ads have an afterglow

Story: you run a pizza shop and spend ₹10,000 on a TV ad on Monday. Some people order that evening. Ramesh sees it Monday but orders on Friday. Priya orders two weeks later when friends visit. The ad sits in people's heads and keeps nudging them — that is "carryover": the effect carries over into future weeks. "Adstock" = the leftover stock of influence the ad still has.

Why the model cares: if it only compared this week's spend to this week's sales, Ramesh's and Priya's orders would look like they came from nowhere, and TV would get too little credit. So we replace "spend this week" with "advertising pressure in the air this week" = fresh spend + fading leftovers from past weeks.

The formula says only that — this week's pressure = fresh spend + α share of last week's pressure:

adstockt = spendt + α · adstockt−1

α is a memory dial (0 to 1). Search ads: α ≈ 0 — someone searching "pizza near me" buys now. TV brand ads: α ≈ 0.6–0.8 — an afterglow lasting weeks. The model learns each channel's dial from the data. Drag the slider and watch the afterglow of one week's spend:

Transform 2

Saturation — the millionth rupee works less hard

Story: your town has 50,000 people. The first ₹1 lakh of ads reaches people who've never heard of you — lots of new customers. The next ₹1 lakh mostly reaches people who already saw the ad or already order from you — fewer new customers. By the fifth lakh, nearly everyone reachable has been reached: extra money buys almost nothing. Same rupee, weaker effect. That's saturation.

So the model doesn't assume "double the spend → double the sales." It fits a curve that rises steeply at first and flattens out. Where a channel currently sits on its curve — steep part (more budget still pays off) or flat part (saturated, money wasted) — is exactly the "where should the next rupee go" answer:

effect = 1 − e−λ·spend
2.00

The dashed line marks where marginal return has dropped to 25% of the first dollar's return — past it, budget is better spent elsewhere. This chart IS the "where should the next dollar go" answer.

Why Bayesian

Small data + correlated channels = you need priors

With ~200 weekly rows and channels that move together, point estimates are unstable. Priors encode marketing knowledge ("TV decay is probably 0.5–0.8"), and the posterior gives honest uncertainty on every ROI.

Interview Drill

Try answering out loud before expanding. If you can't, that's your study list.

1. Why MMM over last-click attribution?
Attribution only sees digital touchpoints, breaks under privacy changes (iOS14, cookie deprecation), and measures correlation of clicks, not incremental sales. MMM uses aggregate data — privacy-proof, covers offline channels (TV, radio, print), and estimates incrementality. Best practice is both: MMM for budget allocation, attribution for in-channel tactics.
2. Why PyMC-Marketing over Robyn or Meridian?
Robyn = ridge regression with evolutionary hyperparameter search — fast, but the search is hard to defend statistically. Meridian = Bayesian and excellent, but its edge is hierarchical geo-level modeling, and my dataset was national-level. PyMC-Marketing gave me a fully transparent Bayesian model: every prior visible, ROI with credible intervals, Python end-to-end.
3. How do you know your model isn't garbage?
Four checks: (1) MCMC convergence — r_hat < 1.01, zero/near-zero divergences; (2) posterior predictive check — simulated sales tracks actual, including holiday spikes; (3) out-of-sample validation on held-out weeks; (4) sanity — ROIs within plausible industry ranges, contributions decompose sensibly (baseline isn't 5% or 95%).
4. ROI vs marginal ROI — what's the difference and which matters?
ROI = average return (total attributed sales ÷ total spend). Marginal ROI = the derivative of the saturation curve at current spend — the return on the next dollar. Budget decisions use marginal: a channel can have great average ROI but be saturated, so its next dollar returns little.
5. What are MMM's limitations?
It's observational, not experimental — correlated spend patterns can bias estimates. Needs 2+ years of weekly data. Struggles with channel collinearity and can't guide fine-grained targeting (audiences, creatives). Gold standard: calibrate MMM with randomized lift tests, then use it for allocation.
6. Two channels always move together — what happens and what do you do?
Multicollinearity: the model can't separate their effects, so posteriors go wide. Fixes: informative priors, combining the channels into one (as I did with print+mail), staggering spend in future planning, or running a lift test on one channel to pin down its effect (calibration).
